Like every other software engineer in the world I am constantly working on some killer idea, most never see the light of day; though normally i do at least learn something from them. Below are a couple of recent ones.

London buses in real time

I challenged myself to create a real time map of buses operating in London, similar to maps that have been created for the London Underground. Since beginning I've realised this is a slightly more ambitious challenge than creating a map of the London Underground. The London Underground can operate 527 trains during rush hour, across 270 stations, which is a lot I'd say. However, the London bus network consists of 7,500 red buses, 19,500 bus stops, along almost 700 routes. Even in the middle of the night there are at least 1000 buses on the road! That is a lot of data to project onto a map...

This raises a lot of issues, from how to locate each bus and how to project it's location onto a bus route. Transport for London provide the required data, in various forms and degrees of usefulness. So far I've been able to locate buses, and map routes but not quite get the overlay of buses on routes correct. I will be soon open sourcing the data I've collated so far and current progress on Github. In the meantime do contact me if you want to know more - I could talk for a while.

Shelter Associates

In 2011 I travelled to India, supported by EWB-UK to work with Shelter Associates in Pune. Shelter Associates are an NGO who work to improve the lives of slum dwellers in Maharastra. During my three months with Shelter I worked on their data system and Drupal based website. The project has progressed since my visit thanks to more recent volunteers, who have worked on improving the slum survey system, and expanding this to allow surveys to be completed on mobile devices which removes the manual process of entering data collected on paper. Shelter is a great example of how Information Technology can be used to make measurable difference in international development.

ICE Toolkit

This is an engineers toolkit for international development which frames common challenges an engineer may face while working in the field, and potential solutions to those challenges. This helps the engineer to respond and also understand the approach required to deal with such challenges. The toolkit was originally planned as a set of printed cards, though it was decided that for a longer lifespan the toolkit would be better as set of content which lived in a CMS of some sort. The end result is a Drupal website containing all of the ~80 cards created by the Institute of Civil Engineers presidents apprentices in 2009-2010. The website was officially launched on the 22nd January 2013 in the House of Commons.
